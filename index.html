Cybersecurity, in
plain terms.

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access, damage, or disruption. It isn't just firewalls and antivirus software — it's a discipline built on three simple questions: who can see this, who can change this, and who can it be trusted to reach when it's needed.

01 The CIA triad

Nearly every security decision traces back to one of these three pillars.

C

Confidentiality

Only the people who are supposed to see data can see it. Encryption, access controls, and strong passwords all serve this pillar.

I

Integrity

Data stays accurate and unaltered unless someone with permission changes it. Checksums and digital signatures protect this.

A

Availability

Systems and data are accessible to authorized users when they need them. Backups and redundancy exist because of this pillar.

02 Common threats you'll actually encounter

Phishing

Fake emails or messages designed to trick you into handing over credentials or clicking a malicious link. Still the most common entry point for breaches.

Malware

Software built to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to a system — viruses, worms, spyware, and trojans all fall under this umbrella.

Ransomware

Malware that encrypts a victim's files and demands payment for the decryption key. Backups are the most reliable defense.

Social engineering

Manipulating people, not systems, into breaking normal security procedure — often more effective than any technical exploit.

Brute-force attacks

Systematically trying passwords until one works. This is exactly what the crack-time estimate on the Password Checker page models.

Man-in-the-middle

An attacker secretly intercepts communication between two parties. HTTPS and encrypted connections exist to close this gap.
